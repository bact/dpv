Abstract

The Data Privacy Vocabulary (DPV) Specification provides the concept Technology to represent how technologies are applied within the context of personal data and its processing. The TECH extension provides further specific details regarding technologies, their management, and relevance to actual real-world tools and systems. The namespace for terms in tech is https://w3id.org/dpv/tech#. The suggested prefix for the namespace is tech. The TECH vocabulary and its documentation is available on GitHub.

2. Core Concepts

The 'core' concepts in the TECH extension consist of:

  • Actor to represent the 'actors' or entities in specific roles such as developing, providing, or using technologies
  • Capability to represent the capabilities or functions of technologies
  • ProvisionMethod to represent how the technolgy is provided - such as services or products
  • CommunicationMechanism to represent how the technology uses or implements communication mechanisms such as networking, GPS, or Bluetooth
  • Documentation to represent documentation associated with technologies such as instructions and user manuals
  • DeploymentLocation to represent the location(s) where the technology is or will be deployed and used
  • IntendedUse to represent the intended use of the technology such as what its developer or provider envisioned the technology will be used for, or how the organisation intends to use the technology

3. Actors involved in Technologies

To specify the specific actors and their roles in the context of technologies, the concept Actor is provided along with the relation hasActor to associate it with the technology. Further specific actors and their associations are defined by extending these concepts, for example:

  • Developer and hasDeveloper specifies who develops or has developed the technology.
  • Provider and hasProvider specifies who provides the technology. This could be the same entity as developer, or a different one - such as when the technology is repackaged or modified before provision.
  • User and hasUser specifies who uses or is the user for the technology.
    • Note: Technology User does not refer to end-user

    The term User refers to users in the technical sense of the term, where such users may or may not be data subjects or end-users or other similar concepts. For example, a technology used by an immigration officer has that officer as the user, rather than the immigrating person. To be more explicit, technology user and whom the technology is used on are two different criterias. See Subject concept to represent the immigrating person in this scenario. In other cases, both the user and subject can be the same person, for example where a person is using a technology on their personal device to take a selfie.

  • Subject and hasSubject is the subject of the technology i.e. whom the technology is used on or subjected to. This may be directly (e.g. person within a CCTV camera's vision) or indirectly (e.g. person whose details were used as training data). What is considered a subject may be contextually dependant on the nature and scope of the technology as well as its application. In the future, we may separate this concept for further distinction between direct and indirect subjects (or use alternate terms) - if such categorisation is deemed beneficial in the description of individuals subjected to technologies.
Note: Technology actors defined in regulations

Technology actors are defined in regulations such as Artificial Intelligence Act (AI Act) and Network Information Security Directive (NIS2) - which have specific criterias and conditions present in the definition and role of such actors. The [TECH] extension takes these regulations as the basis to develop its concepts but provides a generic vocabulary that can be used across jurisdictions and in any-case. To specifically represent an actor as defined within a regulation, the appropriate extension should be used - for example the [EU-AIAct] extension for [AIAct] which contains concepts such as AI Provider and AI Developer as defined in the regulation.

4. Intended Use

Note: Why 'Intended Use' is needed instead of reusing 'Purpose'

Technology is developed and used with a specific purpose or goal in mind - such as to verify identity, or collect specific data, or send information packets over a networking connection. It is important to distinguish such 'purposes' associated with technology from the similar named term 'purpose' in [DPV]. The DPV term Purpose refers to the end-goal and a sufficiently clear description for why the personal data is being processed or the technology is being used. If we use the same concept to indicate the purpose of technologies as described earlier, it would 'pollute' the DPV's purpose taxonomy with other non-purpose concepts such as store data (processing), networking encryption (technical measure), and so on. To avoid this, the TECH extension recommends NOT using the purpose concept directly with technology.

To indicate the uses for which a technology is developed, or what it is planned to be used for, the TECH extension provides the concept IntendedUse and which is associated with using the relation hasIntendedUse. The 'intended use' of technology represents uses which an actor envisions the technology to be used for. This could be the developer who envisions specific uses of the technology while developing it, or a user who plans to use the technology for specific activities when they acquire it. Such intended uses can be represented by using concepts within the DPV and its extensions - such as purposes, processing operations, technical and organisational measures, and so on. Where the intended use cannot be represented by a single concept, multiple concepts can be combined by using the relations in a manner similar to the use of dpv:Process.

Example 2: Indicating the intended uses of technologies

This example indicates the intended use of technology by directly reusing an existing DPV concept (ex:1), and by combining multiple concepts together (ex:2).

ex:1 a dpv:Technology ;
  tech:hasIntendedUse dpv:Collect, dpv:Store ;
  rdfs:comment "Technology intended to collect and store data"@en .

ex:2 a dpv:Technology ;
  tech:hasIntendedUse [
    a tech:IntendedUse ;
    dpv:hasPersonalData pd:EmailAddress ;
    dpv:hasPurpose dpv:Marketing ;
    dpv:hasProcessing dpv:Collect, dpv:Store, dpv:Use ;
    dpv:hasUser dpv:Employee ;
    rdfs:comment "Technology intended to send makreting emails by employee"@en ;
  ] .

Intended Use is an intent or ex-ante indication or plan for the use of technology. The actual use of the technology should be represented within the context of a process instance. Where there is a difference between the intended and actual use, whether such differences are classified as 'misuses' or 'violations' depends on the context. For example if the technology has the intended use to send emails for account communications, and the actual use involved using the technology for sending newsletters, then the difference between intended and actual use may be classified as 'misuse' or it may lead to realising that the earlier intended use did not sufficiently represent all required uses of the technology, and lead to updating the intended uses. Thus, in practice, a technology may have several intended uses that evolve over time.

Example 3: Intended and actual uses of technologies

The following example demonstrates the distinction between intended and actual uses of technologies. The intended use is indicated as a plan or ex-ante process, and the actual use is indicated by associating the technology directly in context of a process. It also demonstrates the ability to directly create an instance of IntendedUse and associate it with multiple technologies by using dpv:hasTechnology. In the example, the intended use of the technologies is represented to be account management, whereas the actual use also involves identity authentication. By comparing the intended with the actual use, it is possible to evaluate whether there are any misuses of the technology or whether the intended uses are incomplete. To enable such uses, it is important to represent the intended and actual uses of technologies in a clear and non-ambigious manner.

ex:TechPlan1 a tech:IntendedUse ;
  dpv:isImplementedUsingTechnology ex:MyDatabase, ex:MyCloudServer ;
  dpv:hasPurpose dpv:AccountManagement .

ex:TechPlan2 a tech:IntendedUse ;
  dpv:isImplementedUsingTechnology ex:MyDatabase ;
  dpv:hasPurpose dpv:IdentityAuthentication .

ex:OperationLog a dpv:Process ;
  dpv:isImplementedUsingTechnology ex:MyDatabase, ex:MyCloudServer ;
  dpv:hasPurpose dpv:AccountManagement ;  # okay as per intended use
  dpv:hasPurpose dpv:IdentityAuthentication . # ambigious use --
          # database has intended use, cloud server doesn't

# a clearer representation
ex:OperationLog a dpv:Process ;
  dpv:hasProcess [ a dpv:Process ;
    dpv:isImplementedUsingTechnology ex:MyDatabase ;
    dpv:hasPurpose dpv:AccountManagement, dpv:IdentityAuthentication ;
  ] ;
  dpv:hasProcess [ a dpv:Process ; 
    dpv:isImplementedUsingTechnology ex:MyCloudServer ;
    dpv:hasPurpose dpv:AccountManagement ;
  ] .

5. Provision of Technology

The provision of technology can happen via the following methods:

  • System, Component, and Algorithmic - that indicate whether the technology is provided as a system (e.g. stand alone), is intended to be used as a component in another system, or is algorithmic (e.g. implementation of a particular method).
  • Service, Goods, Product, and Subscription - that provide an indication of whether the technology is service (e.g. implemented to function as a service), or is a product or a subscription (e.g. provided by a vendor). In this, Service is extended to represent various kinds of CloudService such as Platform-as-a-Service (PaaS) and Software-as-a-Service (SaaS)
  • FixedUse indicates whether the technology has limitations of use e.g. specific number of application or single application, or those related to scale or scope. For example, a single use identifier or a one time scan for vulnerabilities.
Example 4: Indicating a technology is provided as a SaaS subscription

The following example contains a technology which is provided as a SaaS subscription.

7. Documentation

Documentation represents information provided with technologies which 'document' or provide explanations or instructions associated with technologies. Documentation is associated with technology by using hasDocumentation, and conversely technology is associated with documentation by using dct:subject. To represent information about who provided the documentation, when, etc. - we recommend using DCMI Metadata Terms (DCT).

Example 6: Indicating instructions associated with use of technology

The following example indicates how documentation containing instructions for using the technology are defined and associated with technologies.

ex:MyDatabase a dpv:Technology ;
  skos:prefLabel "My Database"@en ;
  tech:hasDocumentation ex:DatabaseDocs .

8. Status

Status of technology, represented by the concept TechnologyStatus, indicates its state of development, deployment, use, and any other relevant stages within the lifecycle. The relation dpv:hasStatus is used to indicate the status of a technology.

Note: Adding additional technology statuses

We welcome contributions and discussions on adding additional statuses to technologies - such as to indicate it is under development, testing, or to indicate it had been released and has been recalled or deprecated or is outdated. We suggest basing such concepts on established standards and authoritative requirements to ensure their practical uses.

Tools are technologies which are commonly used or required, and for which providing a concept within the [TECH] extension would be helpful for representing practical use-cases.
